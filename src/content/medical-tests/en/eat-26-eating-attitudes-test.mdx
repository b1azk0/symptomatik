  - heading: "Eating Disorders: An Overview"
    body: "Eating disorders are serious mental illnesses with biological, psychological, and social factors. They involve disordered eating patterns and distorted body image. Common eating disorders include anorexia nervosa (restrictive eating), bulimia nervosa (binge-purge cycles), and binge eating disorder. Eating disorders have the highest mortality rate of any psychiatric condition. Screening and early intervention are essential."
  - heading: "EAT-26 Assessment Dimensions"
    body: "The EAT-26 measures dieting (restrictive eating attitudes and behaviors), bulimia/food preoccupation (concerns about binge eating and food thoughts), and oral control (self-discipline about food). Higher scores on these dimensions suggest increased eating disorder risk. The assessment helps identify specific eating-related concerns that warrant professional evaluation."
  - heading: "Signs and Symptoms of Eating Disorders"
    body: "Eating disorder warning signs include significant weight changes, preoccupation with food and weight, rigid eating rules, social withdrawal, and excessive exercise. Physical symptoms may include fatigue, dizziness, cold sensitivity, and gastrointestinal problems. Psychological signs include perfectionism, low self-esteem, and anxiety. Early recognition enables intervention."
  - heading: "Health Consequences of Eating Disorders"
    body: "Eating disorders cause serious medical complications including malnutrition, electrolyte imbalances, heart problems, bone loss, and organ damage. Mental health comorbidities such as depression and anxiety are common. The longer an eating disorder persists, the greater the health risks. Early treatment significantly improves outcomes."
  - heading: "Treatment and Recovery Resources"
    body: "Eating disorder treatment is multidisciplinary, involving medical care, nutrition counseling, and psychotherapy. Cognitive-behavioral therapy and family-based treatment are evidence-based approaches. Medical monitoring ensures nutritional rehabilitation and health restoration. Support groups provide community and accountability. Recovery is possible with comprehensive, professional treatment."
