    body: "Prolactin test results are interpreted against standard reference ranges and clinical context: mildly elevated levels can reflect pregnancy, breastfeeding, stress, or certain medications, while markedly high levels may signal a prolactinoma or other pituitary disorder; low levels are less common but can indicate pituitary dysfunction. Factors such as time of day, recent stress or exercise, and medications can skew results, so abnormal values typically prompt repeat testing, thyroid evaluation, medication review, and targeted imaging or endocrine referral to guide treatment and address fertility, menstrual, or metabolic effects."
  - heading: "What is Prolactin and Its Physiological Roles?"
    body: "Prolactin is a peptide hormone secreted by the anterior pituitary that primarily stimulates mammary gland development and milk production after childbirth, but it also has broader physiological roles including modulation of reproductive function (by inhibiting GnRH and affecting ovulation and libido), regulation of immune responses, influence on fluid and electrolyte balance, effects on bone metabolism, and contributions to maternal behaviors; its secretion is stimulated by TRH, estrogen, pregnancy, and suckling and is tonically inhibited by hypothalamic dopamine, making prolactin levels sensitive to physiological states (pregnancy, lactation), stress, medications, and pituitary pathology."
  - heading: "Normal Prolactin Values & Sex Variations"
    body: "Normal prolactin values vary by sex and physiological state: typical reference ranges for nonpregnant adults are roughly 5–25 ng/mL for women and 2–18 ng/mL for men (lab-specific ranges vary), with markedly higher levels in pregnancy and lactation. Women of reproductive age usually have higher baseline prolactin than men, which can affect menstrual function and fertility; mildly elevated levels may reflect pregnancy, stress, or medications, while very high levels suggest a prolactinoma or pituitary disorder."
  - heading: "Elevated Prolactin: Causes & Symptoms"
    body: "Elevated prolactin can result from physiological states (pregnancy, breastfeeding, stress, sleep), hypothyroidism, chest wall stimulation, certain medications (antipsychotics, antidepressants, antiemetics, opioids), and pituitary disorders such as prolactin-secreting adenomas; less commonly it follows renal failure or systemic illness. Symptoms depend on sex and severity and often include galactorrhea, menstrual irregularities or amenorrhea and infertility in women, and decreased libido, erectile dysfunction, or infertility in men, while larger pituitary tumors may cause headaches and visual field defects."
  - heading: "Low Prolactin & Lactation Issues"
    body: "Low prolactin is uncommon but can cause inadequate lactation or failure to establish breastfeeding; causes include pituitary insufficiency (e.g., Sheehan syndrome, surgery, radiation), hypothalamic dysfunction, dopamine agonist therapy, severe stress/illness, or insufficient glandular breast tissue. Evaluation focuses on confirming low levels, assessing other pituitary hormones, reviewing medications, and pituitary MRI when indicated. Management targets the underlying cause, endocrine referral, lactation support and counseling, and—when appropriate and under medical supervision—use of galactagogues (e.g., metoclopramide or domperidone off‑label) to improve milk production."
