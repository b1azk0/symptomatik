  - heading: "What is Anxiety?"
    body: "Anxiety is a normal emotional response to stress or perceived danger characterized by worry, nervousness, and physiological signs like racing heart, muscle tension, or sleep disturbance; it becomes a disorder when these feelings are excessive, persistent, disproportionate to the situation, and interfere with daily functioning. Types range from brief situational anxiety to chronic conditions such as generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, social anxiety, and specific phobias, and causes include genetics, brain chemistry, personality, and life experiences. Recognizing the difference between everyday worry and clinically significant anxiety is important because untreated anxiety can impair work, relationships, and physical health, but effective interventions—psychotherapy, medication, lifestyle changes, and self-help strategies—can substantially reduce symptoms and improve quality of life."
  - heading: "How to Assess the Severity of Anxiety?"
    body: "Assessing anxiety severity involves combining self-report screening (e.g., GAD-7), clinical interview, and observation to quantify symptom frequency, intensity, duration, and functional impact; clinicians screen for key symptoms (excessive worry, restlessness, sleep disturbance, concentration problems, physical signs), assess impairment in work/relationships, rule out medical or substance causes, evaluate risk factors like suicidal ideation or panic symptoms, and consider comorbid mood disorders—mild scores may be managed with psychoeducation and lifestyle changes, moderate to severe scores typically prompt psychotherapy and/or medication and a safety plan, and any rapid deterioration or safety concerns warrant urgent professional care."

    body: "Take the GAD-7 when you notice ongoing or worsening anxiety symptoms—persistent, excessive worry, restlessness, sleep or concentration problems, irritability, or physical signs (racing heart, muscle tension) that last for days to weeks and interfere with work, school, relationships, or daily activities; after a major stressor or life change; if you’ve had anxiety in the past and want to monitor recurrence or treatment progress; when a clinician or primary care provider recommends screening; or anytime symptoms escalate or you have thoughts of harming yourself (in which case seek immediate care rather than waiting for a screening)."

  - heading: "Anxiety Disorder: Indications, Testing & Diagnosis"
    body: "Indications for evaluating an anxiety disorder include persistent, excessive worry or fear, restlessness, irritability, sleep or concentration problems, muscle tension, panic attacks, or physical symptoms (racing heart, shortness of breath) that are disproportionate to circumstances and cause functional impairment or distress for days to weeks; screening is also indicated after major life stressors, recurrence of symptoms, or any safety concerns (suicidal ideation). Testing and assessment typically combine validated self-report measures (e.g., GAD-7, PHQ-9 for comorbid depression), a structured clinical interview and mental-status exam, and targeted medical/laboratory evaluation to exclude thyroid disease, anemia, substance use, or medication effects. Diagnosis is based on symptom type, severity, duration, and functional impact, consideration of differential and comorbid conditions (depression, panic, PTSD, substance use), and risk assessment; mild cases may begin with psychoeducation and lifestyle interventions, while moderate–severe or high-risk presentations warrant psychotherapy, pharmacotherapy, and safety planning."
