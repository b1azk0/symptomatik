    body: "Assessing loneliness rests on understanding both the subjective and objective aspects of social isolation, which is critical for identifying people who need support. Diagnosing loneliness often involves self-report scales such as the UCLA Loneliness Scale or the De Jong Gierveld Loneliness Scale, which measure the level of subjective loneliness in an individual. The UCLA Loneliness Scale (Version 3) is the most widely used tool, consisting of 20 items rated on a 4-point scale; total scores range from 20 to 80, with higher scores indicating greater loneliness. Cut-offs are not universally fixed, but scores in roughly the 28-43 range are commonly considered moderate, 44-55 moderately high, and 56 or above high loneliness. Additional methods include interviews, questionnaires, and observation, which help identify gaps in social networks and assess their quality. A key element of diagnosis is also analysis of risk factors — life changes, social isolation, health problems — that can affect feelings of loneliness. Proper assessment supports tailoring of intervention and support, aimed at improving quality of life for those experiencing loneliness."
  - heading: "What Is Loneliness?"
    body: "Loneliness is a subjective emotional experience that can occur even in the presence of others. It is a state in which an individual feels a lack of deep emotional connection and social support, leading to a sense of alienation and isolation. It is worth noting that loneliness differs from physical isolation; you can feel lonely in a crowd if you lack a sense of belonging or closeness with others. Loneliness can be short-term, tied to a specific life situation, but it can also become chronic, with long-term effects on mental and physical health.\n\nCountering loneliness requires understanding its multidimensional nature and considering the individual needs of each person. Interventions can include both emotional support and practical actions aimed at strengthening social networks. These actions can include organising support groups, individual or group therapy, and community initiatives aimed at social integration. It is also key to promote skills for forming and maintaining interpersonal relationships, which can substantially reduce feelings of loneliness. Cooperation with psychologists, therapists, and community organisations can be invaluable in supporting people experiencing loneliness, helping them build lasting, satisfying relationships and improve quality of life."
