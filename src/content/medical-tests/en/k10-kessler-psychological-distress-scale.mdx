  - heading: "Understanding Psychological Distress"
    body: "Psychological distress encompasses a range of emotional, cognitive, and behavioral responses—such as anxiety, depression, irritability, sleep disturbance, and difficulty concentrating—that commonly arise after illness, major life changes, or prolonged stress; routine screening with validated tools (e.g., PHQ-9, GAD-7, Distress Thermometer) helps detect problems early, clarify severity and risk, and identify patients who will benefit from psychosocial interventions, brief counseling, medication, or referral to specialty mental health services. Screening is typically applied across care settings—primary care, oncology, chronic disease clinics, and community programs—and often reveals mild-to-moderate symptoms that can be managed with stepped care while flagging severe or suicidal ideation for urgent assessment. By translating scores into targeted clinical assessment and individualized care plans, distress screening supports better symptom management, adherence, quality of life, and overall clinical outcomes."
  - heading: "How to Interpret Your K10 Test Results"
    body: "K10 scores range from 10 to 50, with different ranges indicating varying levels of psychological distress. A score of 10-15 suggests minimal distress, 16-21 indicates mild symptoms, 22-29 reflects moderate distress, and 30-50 suggests severe psychological distress. These results provide a general indication of your current mental health status but should not be considered a clinical diagnosis. If you score in the moderate to severe range, it's recommended that you consult with a mental health professional for a comprehensive evaluation and personalized treatment plan. Remember, seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness."
  - heading: "When to Seek Professional Mental Health Support"
    body: "While self-assessment tools like the K10 are valuable for initial screening, they are not substitutes for professional diagnosis. If you consistently score in the moderate to severe range, experience persistent symptoms over several weeks, or feel that your mental health is affecting your daily functioning, it's important to reach out to a qualified mental health professional. A psychologist, psychiatrist, or licensed therapist can provide a thorough assessment, offer evidence-based treatments, and develop a personalized plan to support your recovery and wellbeing. Many mental health conditions are highly treatable when addressed early."
