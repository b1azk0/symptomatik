  - heading: "Borrelia: indications, preparation, procedure, potential side effects"
    body: "Borrelia testing is a key diagnostic tool in Lyme disease — a tick-borne infection caused by spirochetes of the Borrelia genus. Indications include, above all, a tick bite — especially when accompanied by clinical symptoms suggesting infection: erythema migrans, fever, joint and muscle pain, fatigue, or lymphadenopathy. The test is also performed in later stages of the disease, when neurological (facial nerve palsy, neuroborreliosis), cardiac (conduction disturbances, myocarditis), or articular (oligoarticular arthritis affecting large joints) manifestations are present. Borrelia diagnostics are also relevant in patients with chronic non-specific complaints and in people occupationally or environmentally exposed to ticks.\n\nPreparation for Borrelia testing does not require special restrictions, but a few rules help ensure a reliable result. Fasting is not necessary. What matters most is the timing of the test relative to potential exposure: IgM antibodies typically appear 2-4 weeks after a tick bite, and IgG after 4-6 weeks or later. Performing the test too early may give a false negative. Inform your clinician about prior antibiotic therapy, vaccinations, autoimmune conditions, and other infections (e.g. EBV, CMV, syphilis) that can cause cross-reactivity. In the case of erythema migrans, diagnosis is based mainly on clinical presentation and treatment is started without waiting for serology.\n\nThe procedure depends on the chosen diagnostic method. The most common approach is a two-tier serologic protocol: in the first step an ELISA test detects IgM and IgG antibodies, and in the case of a positive or equivocal result, a confirmatory Western blot is performed. The blood sample is drawn from a peripheral vein, usually in the antecubital fossa; the procedure takes a few minutes. In specific situations (neuroborreliosis), cerebrospinal fluid is also tested. Molecular PCR tests and culture methods are increasingly used, although their sensitivity can be limited. Serology results are typically available within 3-7 days and should be interpreted in clinical context — a positive antibody titer does not always indicate active infection.\n\nPotential side effects of Borrelia testing are limited to typical issues associated with venous blood collection. These may include: a small bruise or hematoma at the puncture site, brief tenderness, mild skin redness, and — in sensitive individuals — dizziness or vasovagal syncope. These symptoms resolve spontaneously within a few days. Allergic reactions to disinfectants are rare. In tests requiring cerebrospinal fluid (lumbar puncture), the risk is higher and may include post-puncture headache, bleeding, or infection, which is why this procedure is performed only when neuroborreliosis is reasonably suspected. The diagnostic benefits of correctly identifying Lyme disease far outweigh the minimal risks associated with a standard blood draw."
