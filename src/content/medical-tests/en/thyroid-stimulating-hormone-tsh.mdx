  - heading: "Normal TSH Ranges and What Results Mean"
    body: "Normal TSH typically ranges from 0.4-4.0 mIU/L, though reference ranges vary by laboratory and age. TSH above 4.0 mIU/L indicates hypothyroidism where thyroid hormones are insufficient, causing fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance. TSH below 0.4 mIU/L indicates hyperthyroidism or overtreatment with thyroid replacement, causing palpitations, anxiety, and weight loss. Some patients feel optimal at TSH below 2.0. Borderline elevated TSH (4-10 mIU/L) may progress to overt hypothyroidism, requiring monitoring."
  - heading: "When TSH Testing is Recommended"
    body: "TSH testing is recommended for symptoms suggesting thyroid dysfunction: fatigue, weight changes, mood alterations, temperature sensitivity, or concentration problems. Baseline TSH screening is appropriate in adults over 35. Women planning pregnancy should verify normal TSH for optimal fetal development. Patients on thyroid replacement need regular TSH monitoring for dose optimization. Family history of thyroid disease warrants screening. TSH monitoring helps detect subclinical thyroid disease before symptom development."
  - heading: "TSH and Thyroid Hormone Relationship"
    body: "TSH and thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) function through negative feedback. When T4/T3 are low, TSH rises to stimulate thyroid hormone production. When T4/T3 are high, TSH suppresses to reduce production. This relationship maintains hormone balance. Dysfunction at pituitary or thyroid levels disrupts this balance. TSH accurately reflects thyroid status in most cases but cannot differentiate primary thyroid disease from pituitary-hypothalamic dysfunction. Combined TSH and free T4 testing provides complete thyroid assessment."
