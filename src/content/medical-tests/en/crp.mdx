  - heading: "What is CRP and What Does it Indicate?"
    body: "C-reactive protein (CRP) is a liver-produced blood protein that rises in response to inflammation; the CRP test is a quick, widely available tool clinicians use to detect and monitor acute infections, inflammatory diseases, and to help assess cardiovascular risk (with high-sensitivity CRP for low-grade risk assessment). Interpreting CRP requires clinical context: mild elevations can reflect low-grade or chronic inflammation, while high levels usually indicate acute or severe inflammatory states; serial measurements and correlation with symptoms, physical exam, and other tests (e.g., ESR, hs-CRP) are often more informative than a single value for guiding diagnosis, treatment decisions, and monitoring response to therapy."
  - heading: "Normal CRP Values & Clinical Reference"
    body: "Normal CRP Values & Clinical Reference: Most standard CRP assays consider <10 mg/L (some labs <5 mg/L) as within the reference range; values 10–40 mg/L often reflect mild–moderate inflammation, 40–100 mg/L more significant inflammatory or infectious processes, and >100 mg/L commonly indicates acute bacterial infection or severe inflammation. For cardiovascular risk assessment, high-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) categories are <1 mg/L (low), 1–3 mg/L (moderate), and >3 mg/L (high). Reference ranges can vary by lab and must be interpreted with clinical context and serial measurements."
  - heading: "Elevated CRP: Acute Inflammation & Infection"
    body: "Markedly elevated CRP levels usually signal acute inflammation or bacterial infection and prompt urgent clinical evaluation; the CRP test is a rapid, widely available marker that—when interpreted with symptoms, exam findings, and other tests (e.g., ESR, cultures, hs-CRP)—helps distinguish acute infectious or severe inflammatory states (values >100 mg/L often seen with major bacterial infection) from milder or chronic inflammation where lower rises are observed."
  - heading: "Chronic Elevation & Cardiovascular Risk"
    body: "Chronic low-grade elevation of CRP—especially hs-CRP in the 1–3 mg/L (moderate) to >3 mg/L (high) range—signals persistent systemic inflammation and is associated with increased risk of atherosclerosis, myocardial infarction, and stroke; clinicians use serial hs-CRP measurements alongside risk factors (lipids, blood pressure, smoking, diabetes) to refine cardiovascular risk stratification and guide preventive measures such as lifestyle modification, optimized risk-factor control, and consideration of statin therapy."
