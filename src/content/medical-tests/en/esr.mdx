h1Text: "The erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) test measures how quickly red blood cells settle at the bottom of a tube over one hour and serves as a simple, widely used marker of inflammation in the body. Clinicians order ESR to help detect, monitor, and gauge the severity of inflammatory conditions—such as infections, autoimmune diseases, and certain cancers—or to track response to treatment, though it is non-specific and often interpreted alongside other tests and clinical findings. Normal ESR values vary by age and sex, so results must be assessed in context; elevated or abnormal readings prompt further evaluation to identify the underlying cause."

    body: "ESR results are typically reported as millimeters per hour and interpreted relative to age- and sex-specific reference ranges: modest elevations may reflect chronic or low-grade inflammation, while markedly high values suggest acute or systemic inflammatory processes such as severe infection, active autoimmune disease, or malignancy.   - heading: "What is ESR and How is it Measured?"
    body: "The erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) is a simple blood test, usually performed by the Westergren method, that measures how far red blood cells fall in a vertical tube over one hour and is reported in millimeters per hour; it serves as a non-specific marker of inflammation used to detect, monitor, and gauge the severity of conditions such as infections, autoimmune diseases, and some malignancies. Because normal ESR varies with age and sex and can be influenced by factors like anemia, pregnancy, and certain medications, results must be interpreted in context and typically alongside CRP, clinical findings, and imaging; modest elevations can reflect chronic or low-grade inflammation while very high values point to acute or systemic inflammatory processes, and serial measurements (trends) are often more clinically useful than a single value."
  - heading: "Normal ESR Values & Age/Sex Variations"
    body: "Normal ESR values are reported in mm/hr and vary by age and sex: a common rule of thumb is an upper limit of roughly age/2 for men and (age+10)/2 for women (e.g., ~20 mm/hr for a 40‑year‑old man, ~25 mm/hr for a 40‑year‑old woman), with younger adults typically having lower values and older adults higher; values can also be elevated by pregnancy, anemia, and certain medications, so results must be interpreted with clinical context and often alongside CRP or serial measurements."
  - heading: "Elevated ESR: Causes & Interpretation"
    body: "An elevated ESR indicates increased inflammation but is non‑specific: common causes include infection (especially severe or systemic), active autoimmune or inflammatory diseases (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis, temporal arteritis), malignancies, and chronic inflammatory states; physiologic and noninflammatory factors such as pregnancy, anemia, advancing age, and certain medications can also raise ESR. Interpretation requires age‑ and sex‑adjusted reference ranges, correlation with symptoms, CRP and other labs or imaging, and often serial measurements—modest elevations suggest chronic or low‑grade inflammation, while very high values point toward acute or widespread inflammatory processes needing prompt evaluation."
  - heading: "Low/Normal ESR & Clinical Significance"
    body: "A low or normal ESR generally suggests absence of a significant systemic inflammatory response and, when concordant with a normal CRP and unremarkable exam, lowers the likelihood of active infection, widespread autoimmune inflammation, or malignancy; however, a normal ESR does not exclude localized infections, early or purely cellular immune responses, neutropenia, or conditions that do not raise fibrinogen, and values can be artificially low in polycythemia or hypofibrinogenemia. Clinical context, other labs (especially CRP), and imaging or repeat testing when symptoms persist are essential for appropriate interpretation."
