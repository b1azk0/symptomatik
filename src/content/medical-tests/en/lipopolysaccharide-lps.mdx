sections:
  - heading: "What is Lipopolysaccharide (LPS)?"
    body: "LPS is a large molecule consisting of lipid and carbohydrate components that forms part of the outer membrane of gram-negative bacteria. These bacteria are common in the human gut microbiota, and under normal conditions, the intact intestinal barrier prevents LPS from entering the bloodstream."
  - heading: "LPS Role in Gut Barrier Dysfunction"
    body: "In conditions characterized by intestinal permeability ('leaky gut'), the tight junctions between intestinal epithelial cells are compromised, allowing bacterial LPS to cross into the bloodstream. This bacterial translocation activates immune responses and chronic inflammation throughout the body."
  - heading: "LPS Levels and Systemic Inflammation"
    body: "Elevated blood LPS triggers the innate immune system through pattern recognition receptors, leading to production of pro-inflammatory cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-6. This metabolic endotoxemia is associated with insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, obesity, and cardiovascular disease."
  - heading: "Testing for LPS in Blood"
    body: "LPS can be measured in blood plasma using specialized assays like the LAL (limulus amebocyte lysate) test. Reference ranges vary by laboratory, with elevated levels typically defined as >5 EU/mL (endotoxin units per milliliter)."
  - heading: "Reducing LPS and Improving Gut Health"
    body: "Reducing LPS involves healing the intestinal barrier through dietary modifications, probiotics, prebiotics, and addressing underlying dysbiosis. Eliminating pro-inflammatory foods, increasing fiber intake, and supporting gut barrier function with compounds like L-glutamine and zinc carnosine can help restore intestinal integrity."
