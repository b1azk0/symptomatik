h1: "Ferritin: Normal Ranges, Results & Clinical Interpretation"
h1Text: "Ferritin is the primary storage form of iron in the body and a key laboratory marker used to assess iron status and inflammation; measuring ferritin helps diagnose iron deficiency, iron overload conditions (like hemochromatosis), and can indicate acute or chronic inflammation when levels are elevated. Normal ferritin ranges vary by age, sex, and lab method—commonly about 20–300 ng/mL for men and 15–200 ng/mL for women—but interpretation requires clinical context, concurrent tests (CBC, serum iron, TIBC, transferrin saturation, CRP), and consideration of factors such as pregnancy, liver disease, infection, or recent supplementation. Low ferritin generally reflects depleted iron stores and guides iron-replacement decisions, while high ferritin may reflect excess iron storage, inflammation, liver injury, or metabolic disease and warrants evaluation for underlying causes. Accurate clinical interpretation integrates symptoms, risk factors, and follow-up testing to guide diagnosis and management."

    body: "Ferritin is the main iron-storage protein and a commonly used lab marker to assess body iron stores and inflammatory states; low ferritin indicates depleted iron reserves and typically prompts evaluation for iron-deficiency anemia, while high ferritin can reflect iron overload (e.g., hemochromatosis), inflammation, liver injury, or metabolic disease.
