  - heading: "Understanding Your PHQ-9 Score"
    body: "PHQ-9 scores range from 0 to 27, with different ranges indicating varying severity levels of depression. A score of 0-4 suggests minimal depression, 5-9 indicates mild symptoms, 10-14 reflects moderate depression, 15-19 shows moderately severe depression, and 20-27 suggests severe depression. These ranges help guide clinical decision-making and treatment recommendations. A score of 10 or higher is generally considered clinically significant and warrants further evaluation and possible treatment. If your score falls in the moderate range or higher, discussing your results with a healthcare provider is recommended."
  - heading: "Is the PHQ-9 Right for Diagnosing Depression?"
    body: "While the PHQ-9 is an excellent screening tool for identifying depression risk, it is not a clinical diagnosis on its own. A formal diagnosis of major depressive disorder requires evaluation by a qualified mental health professional or physician who can conduct a comprehensive assessment, rule out other medical conditions, and consider your full clinical picture. The PHQ-9 serves as a starting point for conversations with healthcare providers and helps establish baseline symptom severity for monitoring progress during treatment. If you score in the moderate to severe range, seeking professional evaluation is the next important step."

    body: "If your PHQ-9 results suggest depression, several evidence-based treatment options are available. Psychotherapy (such as cognitive behavioral therapy), medication (antidepressants), lifestyle modifications, or a combination of these approaches have strong research support for treating depression. Your healthcare provider can help determine the most appropriate treatment based on symptom severity, medical history, and personal preferences. Early intervention is associated with better outcomes, and many people experience significant improvement with proper treatment. Taking action after recognizing depression symptoms is a positive step toward recovery."
