  - heading: "Understanding PSQI Scores and Global Sleep Quality"
    body: "PSQI global scores range from 0 to 21, with higher scores indicating worse sleep quality. A score of 5 or less is generally considered good sleep quality, while scores above 5 suggest poor sleep quality and possible sleep disorders such as insomnia. The questionnaire allows for detailed analysis of specific sleep problems, such as difficulty falling asleep, frequent nighttime awakenings, or insufficient sleep duration. Your PSQI score can be used to track changes in sleep quality over time and to assess the effectiveness of sleep improvement interventions. If your score indicates poor sleep quality, consulting with a sleep specialist or healthcare provider is recommended."
  - heading: "Common Sleep Disorders and When to Seek Professional Help"
    body: "Common sleep disorders include insomnia (difficulty falling or staying asleep), sleep apnea (breathing interruptions during sleep), restless leg syndrome, and narcolepsy. If the PSQI indicates poor sleep quality, especially if accompanied by daytime fatigue, difficulty concentrating, mood problems, or safety concerns (such as drowsy driving), professional evaluation is important. A sleep medicine specialist can conduct further testing, such as a sleep study, to identify specific sleep disorders. Many sleep disorders are highly treatable with appropriate interventions, which may include behavioral techniques, medical devices, medication, or treatment of underlying health conditions."
  - heading: "Behavioral Strategies for Improving Sleep Quality"
    body: "Before pursuing medical interventions, behavioral changes can significantly improve sleep quality. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is the gold standard non-medication treatment and focuses on changing sleep-related thoughts and behaviors. Key strategies include maintaining a consistent sleep-wake schedule, limiting time in bed to actual sleep time, avoiding naps, and creating a cool, dark, quiet sleep environment. Limiting caffeine, alcohol, and large meals close to bedtime, exercising regularly but not close to sleep, and managing stress through relaxation techniques all support better sleep. A wind-down routine 30-60 minutes before bed, involving relaxing activities and reduced screen exposure, helps prepare your body for sleep."
  - heading: "Creating an Optimal Sleep Environment and Lifestyle"
    body: "Your sleep environment plays a crucial role in sleep quality. The ideal bedroom temperature is around 65-68 degrees Fahrenheit, with minimal light and noise. Consider blackout curtains, a white noise machine, and a comfortable mattress and pillow. Exposure to natural light during the day, particularly in the morning, helps regulate your circadian rhythm. Limiting blue light from screens at least one hour before bed is important, as it can suppress melatonin production. Regular physical exercise improves sleep quality, but should be completed at least 3-4 hours before bedtime. Managing stress through meditation, journaling, or relaxation techniques prepares your mind for sleep. These environmental and lifestyle factors provide a foundation for better sleep that works best when combined with professional support if needed."
