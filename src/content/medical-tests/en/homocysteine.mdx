h1Text: "Homocysteine is an amino acid formed during methionine metabolism. Elevated homocysteine (hyperhomocysteinemia) is an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and venous thromboembolism. sections:
  - heading: "Understanding Homocysteine as a Risk Factor"
    body: "Elevated homocysteine damages endothelial cells, promotes atherosclerosis, and increases thrombotic risk. It oxidizes LDL cholesterol and interferes with anticoagulant mechanisms. Homocysteine levels increase with age and are higher in men than women."
  - heading: "Elevated Homocysteine and Heart Disease"
    body: "High homocysteine is associated with increased risk of myocardial infarction, stroke, and atherosclerosis. The risk increases substantially when homocysteine exceeds 15 μmol/L. Even moderately elevated levels (12-15 μmol/L) may increase cardiovascular risk."
  - heading: "Homocysteine Normal Ranges and Interpretation"
    body: "Normal fasting homocysteine levels are typically below 15 μmol/L, with optimal levels below 10 μmol/L. Levels between 15-30 μmol/L indicate moderate elevation, 30-100 μmol/L represent intermediate elevation, and levels above 100 μmol/L indicate severe homocysteinemia."
  - heading: "Causes of Elevated Homocysteine"
    body: "Elevated homocysteine results from deficiencies in B vitamins (B6, B12, folate), genetic mutations in homocysteine metabolism enzymes, renal disease, hypothyroidism, certain medications, and lifestyle factors like smoking and excessive caffeine intake."
  - heading: "Lowering Homocysteine with B Vitamins"
    body: "Supplementation with folate (1-5 mg daily), B12 (500-2000 mcg daily), and B6 (25-100 mg daily) can significantly reduce elevated homocysteine. Testing methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) mutations may guide supplementation choices. Response to therapy should be monitored with repeat testing."
