  - heading: "What is C-Reactive Protein and Why It Matters"
    body: "C-Reactive Protein (CRP) is a protein produced by the liver that enters the bloodstream during inflammation or infection. Unlike other markers, CRP can rise and fall relatively quickly in response to acute conditions, making it a dynamic indicator of inflammation. Understanding CRP levels helps identify inflammatory processes in the body early, which is crucial for preventing chronic diseases and maintaining overall health."
  - heading: "C-Reactive Protein as an Inflammation Marker"
    body: "As an acute phase reactant, CRP responds rapidly to tissue injury, infection, and inflammation. It binds to phosphocholine on pathogens and damaged cells, facilitating their removal by immune cells. Elevated CRP indicates systemic inflammation but is not specific to any particular disease. It can be elevated in rheumatoid arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, cardiovascular disease, infections, and even after physical stress or trauma."
  - heading: "C-Reactive Protein Reference Values & Interpretation"
    body: "Normal CRP levels in adults typically fall below 3-5 mg/L, though exact ranges may vary by laboratory and testing method. Results between 5 and 10 mg/L may suggest mild inflammation, results between 10 and 100 mg/L indicate significant inflammation, and results above 100 mg/L suggest severe inflammation or infection. However, CRP elevation is not specific to any particular disease, so it must always be interpreted in clinical context."
  - heading: "When to Test Your CRP Levels"
    body: "CRP testing is recommended for patients with symptoms of infection or inflammation, those with chronic inflammatory conditions, individuals with cardiovascular risk factors, or those recovering from surgery or trauma. High-sensitivity CRP (hs-CRP) is increasingly used to assess cardiovascular disease risk in seemingly healthy individuals."
  - heading: "Clinical Significance of CRP Results"
    body: "Elevated CRP serves as an independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease and stroke, even in the absence of other risk factors. In chronic inflammatory conditions, CRP can be used to monitor disease activity and treatment response. Serial CRP measurements over time can help track the course of infection or recovery from illness."
